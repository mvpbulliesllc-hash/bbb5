## The symptoms we get called for

- **Limb strikes** — oaks drop branches in every summer thunderstorm and winter nor'easter; the visible dent often sits over cracked decking
- **Ceiling stains** below skylights and near chimneys — nearly always a flashing failure, not "bad shingles"
- **Clogged, overflowing valleys** — leaves and acorns dam the dead valleys on these complex colonials and push water under the courses
- **Wind-lifted or missing shingles** on exposed estate-corridor rooflines off Route 537 and Route 34
- **Ice-dam leaks** at the eaves each winter, worst over unheated garage wings and cathedral great rooms

## How we diagnose it

We don't smear tar and leave. We trace water to its actual entry point — often feet uphill from where the stain shows — check the decking around any limb strike, pull and re-work flashing rather than caulking over it, and photograph the whole roof by drone so you see exactly what we found. On the older farmhouses and capes in Scobeyville and Vanderburg, we also check for plank-sheathing rot that a surface patch would just hide.

## The honest fix-or-replace call

A repair is the right move when the roof is under 15–18 years old, the shingle field is otherwise sound, and the damage is isolated to one area. But if your Colts Neck colonial is on its original 25–30-year roof, shedding granules everywhere, and you're chasing your third leak in two winters, patching is money down a well — a [full replacement](/roof-replacement/colts-neck/) is cheaper over any ten-year window.

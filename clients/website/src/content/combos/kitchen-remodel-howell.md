## What drives the price in Howell's kitchens

Howell's housing stock breaks into a few distinct eras, and each one changes what a kitchen remodel actually involves:

- **Late-60s and 70s splits and ranches in Candlewood and Ramtown** — These kitchens are often small and closed off from the rest of the house, with original layouts that predate open-concept living. Jobs here frequently include removing a partition wall, relocating an outdated electrical panel feed, or adding recessed lighting where there was none. Cabinet and counter work alone stays toward the lower end of the range; opening the layout pushes it up.
- **1980s–90s colonials along the Route 9 corridor** — These kitchens are usually already a reasonable size but carry builder-grade oak cabinets, laminate counters, and dated soffits. Remodels here tend to be bigger-ticket: full cabinet replacement, quartz or granite counters, an island addition, and updated lighting and outlets to meet current code.
- **Newer construction and 55+ communities** — Layouts are already open, so work often focuses on finish upgrades — counters, backsplash, cabinet fronts, and appliance packages — rather than structural changes.

## Can you open up my closed-off kitchen layout?

Often, yes — but not every wall in a Howell kitchen can move. Some are load-bearing and require an engineered header, which adds cost and a structural detail to the permit set; others are simple partitions that come down in an afternoon. We walk your kitchen before you commit to a design, tell you honestly which walls can go, and build layout options around what your house can actually support, rather than selling a Pinterest layout that runs into a structural wall three weeks into demo. This comes up most in Candlewood and Ramtown splits, where original layouts often wall the kitchen off entirely.

## What breaks first on a Point Pleasant roof

Salt air and channeled wind fail specific parts in a predictable order:

- **Pipe boots and flashing** — galvanized metal and neoprene collars corrode and crack years early this close to the canal and creek. The single most common leak we fix in the borough.
- **Wind-lifted or creased shingles** — nor'easter gusts funnel up the Manasquan River and along the canal; east-facing slopes in Bay Head Shores and Lovelandtown take the worst of it.
- **Fatigued sealant strips** — constant wind cycling breaks the factory bond, so tabs flutter and eventually tear even when the roof looks fine from the street.
- **Nail pops and worn field shingles** — the borough's 1950s–70s capes, ranches, and split-levels are mostly on their second or third roof, and the oldest cycle is due.

## How we find the actual leak

A ceiling stain almost never sits under the hole. Water enters at a penetration or flashing joint, runs along the underlayment or a rafter, and surfaces rooms away. We inspect from the attic and the roof — drone included when slopes are steep or wet — trace the path, and hand you photos of exactly what we found with a line-item price for the fix. No "while we're up there" padding.

## Repair or replace: the honest call

A $600 flashing fix on a 10-year-old roof is easy money. Three repairs a year on a 22-year-old roof is paying replacement price on the installment plan. Our rule of thumb: if the roof has five or more good years left and the problem is localized, repair it. If failures are spreading across the field — and salt exposure here shaves 3–5 years off a shingle's life to begin with — put the money toward [a replacement](/roof-replacement/point-pleasant/) instead; [financing](/financing/) turns it into a monthly number.

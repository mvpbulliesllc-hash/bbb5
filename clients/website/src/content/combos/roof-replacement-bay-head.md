## Two waterfronts, one spec

Bay Head is a few blocks wide almost everywhere, so salt doesn't approach your roof from one side — it wraps it. Our replacement spec assumes that:

- Full tear-off to the deck — never a layover over tired shingles
- Deck inspection with rot swapped and **photographed before it's covered**
- Ice & water shield at eaves, valleys, and dormer cheeks; synthetic underlayment everywhere else
- 130 mph architectural shingles, six nails each, hand-sealed edges on exposed slopes
- Stainless or hot-dipped fasteners and new flashing throughout — reused flashing is how coastal roofs fail early
- Ridge venting matched to your attic's actual intake

Weighing shingle lines? Our guide to [the best shingles for the Jersey Shore](/blog/best-shingles-jersey-shore/) covers what survives here.

## Built-in-1895 is a different job than built-in-2015

We roof every era Bay Head has: the **shingle-style cottages (1880s–1920s)** of the historic core, where turrets, stacked porch roofs, and dormers pack more flashing per square than any modern build; the **East Avenue oceanfront** homes taking direct Atlantic wind, where edge sealing earns its keep; and the **post-Sandy elevated rebuilds**, which need uplift resistance the original house never did. Want the weathered-cedar look the borough is known for? Natural shake, synthetic shake, and shake-profile asphalt are all on the table — with honest numbers for each.

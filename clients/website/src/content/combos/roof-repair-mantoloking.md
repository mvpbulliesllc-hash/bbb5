## How trouble shows up, house by house

Three kinds of Mantoloking roof, three failure patterns:

- **The 1880s–1920s estates on Ocean Avenue** — the shingle-style and gambrel rooflines pack more valleys, dormer sidewalls, and flashing transitions into one house than a block of mainland ranches. A stain on a third-floor ceiling under a dormer is the classic call, along with cracked or cupped cedar on the weather side.
- **The 2013–2016 rebuild-wave roofs** — a huge share of the borough re-roofed after Sandy, and those roofs are now a decade-plus into hard salt exposure. That's the age when sealant beads split, exposed fasteners back out, and edge details quit while the shingles still look fine from the street.
- **Barnegat Lane and the bayfront** — constant moisture instead of raw wind: algae-streaked north slopes, corroded fasteners, and flashing that failed quietly years before anyone saw water inside.

Elevated rebuilds add one more: caps and rake shingles in the yard after a blow, because the raised house catches wind the original never did.

Our rule is simple: localized damage on a roof with five-plus good years left gets repaired; spreading failure means every fix is rent.

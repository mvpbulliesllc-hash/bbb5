## What Beachwood roofs do when they fail

Different sides of the borough break differently, and your street name usually tells us what we'll find:

- **River blocks near Beachwood Beach West and the Yacht Club** — tabs lifted or creased after a nor'easter pushes up the Toms River, plus flashing and fasteners corroding years early in the salt air
- **Pre-war bungalows near the waterfront** — leaks where low-slope porch and dormer additions ask shingles to shed water at a pitch they were never made for
- **Capes, ranches, and split-levels on the central grid, Pinewald Road, and the Jakes Branch side** — chimney and vent-pipe flashing giving out on second- or third-cycle roofs, moss on shaded slopes, and ice dams over needle-packed gutters after a hard winter

## How we find the actual leak

The ceiling stain almost never sits under the hole — water travels along rafters and plank decking before it shows. We check the attic side first, fly the drone over the slope and flashing, and follow the moisture path to its entry point. You get photos of exactly what failed and an itemized quote for exactly that fix. No "while we're up here" padding.

## When we'll tell you to stop repairing

We fix roofs with life left in them, and we say so plainly when one doesn't. The signals we act on: shingles that snap instead of flex, a second repair to the same slope inside two years, or widespread soft decking on an older bungalow. Past that point, repair money is rent, not equity.

## A spec that respects a 1910s beach colony

Beachwood was sold off as a newspaper-promotion summer colony, and the blocks near Beachwood Beach West still carry the proof: winterized 1920s–40s bungalows with plank decking, two or three old shingle layers, and low-slope porch additions grafted on over the decades. Our replacement spec is built around what we actually find up there:

- **Every layer comes off.** A layover on a Beachwood bungalow just hides plank gaps and pre-loads the next leak.
- **Plank decking repaired or overlaid** so the new shingles have solid nailing.
- **130 mph wind-rated shingles, six nails each** — the river mouth funnels nor'easter wind straight at the waterfront and the Beachwood Boulevard corridor.
- **Corrosion-resistant drip edge and flashing**, because salt air off the Toms River eats builder-grade metal.
- **Ventilation corrected.** Most of these houses were never built to be heated, and their attics still act like it.

On the 1950s–70s capes, ranches, and split-levels inland toward Pinewald Road and the Route 9 side, the job is more conventional — but the pine cover near Jakes Branch County Park means we upsize valley protection and check gutter capacity while we're up there.

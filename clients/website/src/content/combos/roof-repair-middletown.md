## What Middletown roofs actually fail from

The symptom you see rarely sits where the leak starts. Here's what we chase down most across town:

- **Wind-lifted and missing shingles** — the classic bayshore call in Belford, Port Monmouth, and Leonardo after a nor'easter, worse on elevated post-Sandy homes
- **Flashing leaks** — chimneys, skylights, dormers, and wall step-flashing on the cut-up Lincroft and Oak Hill rooflines, where water sneaks in feet from the stain inside
- **Cracked pipe boots** — the single most common leak we find, cheap and quick once it's spotted
- **Valley wear** — the busiest water path on any roof, first to thin out on 1950s–70s split-levels in New Monmouth and River Plaza
- **Moss and algae holding moisture** — shaded wooded lots in Navesink and Locust, where damp shingles rot the deck underneath

## How we track the source

We don't guess from the driveway. We get on the roof, check the attic side where we can, and follow the water uphill from the stain to where it's really entering — a boot, a flashing gap, a nail pop, a wind-opened seam. On shaded Navesink roofs we also probe the decking, because moss that's been sitting for years often means the real repair is the plywood, not the shingle. You get a photo of the actual problem, by drone when the pitch calls for it, before we touch anything.

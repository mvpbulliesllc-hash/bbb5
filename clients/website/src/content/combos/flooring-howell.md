## Matching the floor to the house

Howell's housing stock spans several eras, and the right flooring choice often depends on which one you are in:

- **Late-60s and 70s splits and ranches (Candlewood, Ramtown)** — Original hardwood or worn carpet over old subfloor is common. These jobs frequently start with subfloor inspection and leveling before new material goes down, since decades of settling can leave floors slightly uneven. Refinishing existing hardwood is often an option here if the original wood is still solid underneath.
- **1980s–90s colonials along the Route 9 corridor** — Builder-grade hardwood or laminate that has worn through the finish is typical. Whole-house LVP or new hardwood replacement is a common upgrade, especially when paired with a kitchen or bath remodel already in progress.
- **55+ communities and single-level homes** — Flooring here often prioritizes a consistent, low-transition surface throughout for aging-in-place living — one material across bedrooms, halls, and living space instead of mixed carpet and tile.

## Wooded lots change the moisture equation

Much of Howell sits on wooded, spread-out lots, and homes near the Manasquan Reservoir, Allaire State Park, or tucked into Southard and West Farms tree cover see more humidity swings through the seasons than an open-lot home would. Solid hardwood can visibly expand and contract with that variation over time. Luxury vinyl plank and porcelain tile are dimensionally more stable in these conditions, which is why we walk you through material trade-offs for your specific lot before you commit, rather than selling one flooring type across every job.

## What flooring holds up best on a wooded Howell lot?

Luxury vinyl plank and porcelain tile generally hold up better than solid hardwood on wooded, humidity-prone lots like those in Southard, Ardena, and West Farms, or near the reservoir. Before any installation, we also check for soft spots, unevenness, and moisture in the subfloor — issues that show up more often in Howell's older splits and ranches than in newer construction. Catching that during the estimate, not mid-installation, is how we keep the job on schedule and the final floor flat and stable.

## Hour zero: the leak is the cheap part

When wind opens a roof over the Jersey Shore, the shingles are rarely the expensive damage — it's the
**water that follows for the next 24–48 hours**: insulation, drywall, flooring, electrical, mold.
The first day decides whether you have a roofing bill or a gut-renovation bill. Here's the playbook.

## The first-24-hours checklist

1. **Stay off the roof.** Wet, wind-damaged decking fails without warning. Work from inside and from the ground.
2. **Control water inside.** Buckets under drips, plastic over furniture, and if a ceiling is bulging, one small screwdriver hole at the low point drains it before the whole ceiling comes down.
3. **Kill power to wet areas.** Water in light fixtures or outlets = breaker off first, questions later.
4. **Photograph everything** — roof opening, each wet room, the fallen limb — before anything is moved or covered. Date-stamped photos are your claim's backbone.
5. **Call for professional tarping.** A real emergency tarp is stretched taut, wrapped over the ridge, and fastened with furring strips and cap nails so it survives the next gust. We tarp active leaks fast — 848-633-6440 — and the invoice is reimbursable on your claim.
6. **Report the loss to your carrier** within days, not weeks. Note the storm date; it becomes your date of loss.
7. **Do not sign anything on your doorstep.** After big storms, out-of-state crews push Assignment-of-Benefits paperwork. Keep control of your claim and use a [licensed local contractor you can verify](/blog/nj-contractor-license-lookup/).

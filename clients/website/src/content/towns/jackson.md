## Trees are Jackson's salt air

At the beach we fight wind and salt. In Jackson the enemy is the canopy. Oaks and pitch pines drop debris that packs valleys and gutter runs, where it holds moisture against the shingles until the mat breaks down early. Shaded slopes grow the black algae streaks and moss you see all over town. In winter those same shaded eaves are where snow lingers and [ice dams form](/blog/ice-dams-winter-roof-problems-nj/), forcing meltwater under the shingles. And nearly every summer thunderstorm that crosses Route 537 drops a limb on somebody's roof.

So we build for it: generous ice & water shield at eaves and valleys, decking checked hard around every debris-collecting penetration, and [gutter protection](/gutters/) recommended on wooded lots as a matter of course. Inland doesn't mean wind-free, either — nor'easter gusts still rake the open stretches of former farmland, so we fasten to high-wind spec here too.

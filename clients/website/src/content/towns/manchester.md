## Trees, heat, and ice — the inland wear pattern

Manchester roofs don't fight salt air. They fight the forest. Pine needles and oak leaves trap moisture on shingles and clog valleys; shaded slopes grow moss that lifts shingle edges; and every nor'easter or summer thunderstorm drops limbs on somebody's ridge. Add sandy, full-sun lots that push attic temperatures well past 130°F in July and long ranch eaves that build [ice dams](/blog/ice-dams-winter-roof-problems-nj/) in a freeze-thaw January, and Manchester shingles earn their retirement honestly.

One more inland reality: the Pine Barrens burn. Manchester saw a serious wildfire off Route 539 in 2023, and every asphalt shingle system we install carries a Class A fire rating — the highest there is — plus we'll flag gutters packed with dry needles while we're up there.

## A town that built itself in twenty years

Until 1975 this was Madison Township, and the name survives on the rooftops: **Madison Park** and **Sayre Woods South** alone put thousands of capes and ranches on former farmland in the 1950s, each street shingled in one pass by the same tract builders. Housing that goes up together wears out together — many of those homes are now on their **third roof cycle**, and what we find under the shingles follows the same script block after block.

## Laurence Harbor: the shoreline nobody expects

On paper Old Bridge is an inland suburb. Then you drive down to **Laurence Harbor** and you're standing on Raritan Bay, looking at the Manhattan skyline from Old Bridge Waterfront Park. That strip began as a 1920s summer bungalow colony, took a beating from Sandy, and still catches every nor'easter that funnels down the bay. Roofs there need what true shore roofs need: 130 mph wind-rated shingles, six-nail fastening, sealed edge detail, and flashing that shrugs off salt air. We spec it that way by default — the full playbook is in our guide to [nor'easter-proofing a coastal roof](/blog/coastal-roofing-noreaster-protection/).

## What wears out the rest of the township

Away from the bay, the enemies are temperature and trees. A 1950s cape with a half-story attic and original insulation is a textbook [ice dam](/blog/ice-dams-winter-roof-problems-nj/) machine — snow melts over the warm living space, refreezes at the cold eave, and backs water under the shingles. Summer runs the attack in reverse, cooking under-vented attics from below. And on the wooded blocks near Cheesequake State Park, oak litter packs valleys and [gutters](/gutters/) every fall and holds moisture against the shingle mat. Every replacement we do here gets generous ice & water shield and a ventilation fix, because those are what killed the last roof.
